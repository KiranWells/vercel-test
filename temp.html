# Stokes' Theorem Recall surface integrals over vector fields: For a vector field \(\vec F\), the flux over a surface \(\textit{S}\) parametrized by \(r(u, v)\) is \[\iint\limits_S \vec F \cdot \,dS = \iint\limits_D \vec F(r(u, v)) \cdot (r_u \times r_v) \,dA\] Recall Green's Theorem: For a vector field \(\vec F\), and a ``nice'' closed curve \(C\) that encloses a simply connected region \(D\), \[\int\limits_C \vec F \cdot \,dr = \oint\limits_C P\,dx + Q\,dy = \iint\limits_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)\,dA\] ## Definition: Stokes' Theorem \[\int\limits_C \vec F \cdot \,dr = \iint\limits_S \mathrm{curl}\ \vec F \cdot \,dS\] Just like Green's Theorem, Stokes' Theorem converts a line integral into a double integral. However, this can be a curve in space! In the case where \(S\) lies along the \(xy\)-plane, this degenerates to Green's Theorem. This means the work done in moving a particle through a vector field along a closed curve is mathematically equivalent to the flux of \(\mathrm{curl}\ \vec F\) over any surface the curve encloses.
Click to show Example 1 ### Question 1 Use Stokes' Theorem to find \(\int_C \vec F \cdot \,dr\) where \(\vec F = \langle z^2, 2x, y^2 \rangle\) and \(C\) is the curve of intersection of the plane \(y + z = 2\) and the cylinder \(x^2 + y^2 = 1\). Orient \(C\) to be counterclockwise when looking from above (which ensures the normal vector points upward). #### Solution \[\int\limits_C \vec F \cdot \,dr = \iint\limits_S \mathrm{curl}\ \vec F \cdot \,dS\] \[\mathrm{curl}\ \vec F = \nabla \times \vec F = \langle \frac{\partial}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial}{\partial y}, \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \rangle \times \langle z^2, 2x, y^2 \rangle\] \[=\det \begin{bmatrix} \hat{i} && \hat{j} && \hat{k} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} && \frac{\partial}{\partial y} && \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ z^2 && 2x && y^2 \end{bmatrix} = \langle 2y, 2z, 2\rangle\] Our domain of integration \(S\) is the surface enclosed by \(C\), the curve of intersection between the plane \(y + z = 2\) and the cylinder \(x^2 + y^2 = 1\). Parametrizing the plane, we have \(r(x, y) = \langle x, y, 2-y \rangle\), and so \[r_x \times r_y = \langle 1, 0, 0 \rangle \times \langle 0, 1, -1 \rangle = \langle 0, 1, 1 \rangle\] Which is positively oriented because \(z > 0\) Now, from the beginning, we have \[=\iint\limits_S \mathrm{curl}\ \vec F \cdot \,dS = \iint\limits_D \mathrm{curl}\ \vec F \left(r(x, y) \cdot (r_x \times r_y)\right) \,dA\] where \(D = \{(x, y) \mid x^2 + y^2 \le 1\} \) is the domain of our parametrization of \(r\). \[=\iint\limits_{x^2 + y^2 \le 1} \langle 2y, 2(y-2), 2 \rangle \cdot \langle 0, -1, 1 \rangle \,dA\] \[=\iint\limits_{x^2 + y^2 \le 1} (6-2y)=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 (6 - 2r \sin \theta) r \,dr \,d\theta\] \[=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 (6 - 2r \sin \theta) r \,dr \,d\theta\] \[=2\pi \cdot \left[ 3r^2 - \frac{2r^3}{3}\sin\theta \right]_{r=0}^{r=1}=6\pi\]
