# Summary, sections 12.1-13.4 This is mostly a list of useful properties and formulas. FOr the concepts behind them, I have provided links to the pages that explain each topic ## [Vectors](vectors.html) Vectors are 3-Dimensional, and represent a point or direction in space: $$\vec v = \langle x,y,z\rangle$$ ### Operations $$-\vec v = \langle -x, -y, -z \rangle$$ $$\vec v + \vec u = \langle x_v + x_u, y_v + y_u, z_v + z_u\rangle$$ $$\vec v \cdot \vec u = x_v * x_u + y_v * y_u + z_v * z_u$$ $$\vec v \times \vec u = \langle y_vz_u - z_vy_u, z_vx_u - x_vz_u, x_vy_u - y_vx_u \rangle $$ ### Properties $$ \vec a \cdot \vec b = |\vec a| * |\vec b| * \cos(\theta) $$ $$ |\vec a \times \vec b| = |\vec a| * |\vec b| * \sin(\theta) $$ Dot product is associative, cross product is not. ### Uses #### Finding perpendicular/parallel vectors $$ iff \nobreakspace\nobreakspace\vec a \cdot \vec b = 0 $$ $$ \vec a \parallel \vec b \nobreakspace\nobreakspace iff \nobreakspace\nobreakspace \vec a \times \vec b = 0 $$ #### Projections Scalar: (the length of the projection) $$ comp_a\vec b = |\vec b| \cos(\theta) = {\vec a \cdot \vec b \over {|\vec a|}} $$ Vector: (the vector representation of the projection) $$ proj_a\vec b = comp_a\vec b * {\vec a \over |\vec a|} = \left({\vec a \cdot \vec b \over {|\vec a|}}\right){\vec a \over |\vec a|} = {\vec a \cdot \vec b \over {|\vec a|^2}}\vec a $$ ### Unit Vectors $$ \hat i = \langle 1,0,0 \rangle $$ $$ \hat j = \langle 0,1,0 \rangle $$ $$ \hat k = \langle 0,0,1 \rangle $$ $$ \vec a = \langle a_1,a_2,a_3 \rangle = a_1 \hat i + a_2 \hat j + a_3 \hat k $$ ## [Lines](planes.html) Lines are infinite, one-dimensional objects that can be described by an equation All lines are curves (but not all curves are lines) Essentially, they are defined by an initial point (like a y-intercept) and a direction/vector (like a slope) that maps $t$ (time) to each point on the line ### Vector Equation $$ \vec r(t) = \vec r_0 + t\vec v $$ $$ \vec r(t) = \langle x_0 + at, y_0 + bt, z_0 + ct\rangle$$ ### Parametric Equation $$ x(t) = x_0 + at $$ $$ y(t) = y_0 + bt $$ $$ z(t) = z_0 + ct $$ ### Symmetric Equations $$ t = {x - x_0 \over a} = {y - y_0 \over b} = {z - z_0 \over c} $$ ### Line Segments $$ r(t) = \vec r_0 + t(\vec r_1 - \vec r_0) = (1 - t)\vec r_0 + t\vec r_1 $$ $$ 0 \le t \le 1 $$ ## [Planes](planes.html) Planes are infinite, two-dimensional objects that can be defined by an equation All planes are surfaces (but not all surfaces are lines) Planes are defined by a vector that is perpendicular to the plane and a point on that plane ### Scalar Equation $$ a(x - x_0) + b(y - y_0) + c(z - z_0) = 0 $$ ### Linear form $$ ax + by + cz = d $$ $$ where $$ $$ d = ax_0 + by_0 + cz_0 $$ ## Curves Curves are equations that describe the movement of a line in space. They are usually parametric, with the form: $$\vec r(t) = \langle f(t), g(t), h(t) \rangle$$ or $$\vec r(t)=\begin{cases} x = f(t) \\ y = g(t) \\ z = h(t) \\ \end{cases}$$ ## [Calculus with vectors and equations in 3D](calculus_of_vectors.html) TL;DR: To take the limit, derivative, or integral of a vector function, do the same to each component function. ### Limits and Continuity $$\lim\limits_{t \to a} \vec r(t) = \langle \lim\limits_{t \to a} f(t), \lim\limits_{t \to a} g(t), \lim\limits_{t \to a} h(t) \rangle$$ The function is continuous if the individual pieces are continuous: $$\lim\limits_{t \to a}f(t) = f(a)$$ $$\lim\limits_{t \to a}g(t) = g(a)$$ $$\lim\limits_{t \to a}h(t) = h(a)$$ ### Derivatives $$\vec r^\prime(t) = \langle f'(t), g'(t), h'(t) \rangle$$ #### Rules for dot and cross products $$\frac{d}{dt}[\vec u(t) \cdot \vec v(t)] = \vec u'(t) \cdot \vec v'(t) + \vec u(t) \cdot \vec v(t)$$ $$\frac{d}{dt}[\vec u(t) \times \vec v(t)] = \vec u'(t) \times \vec v'(t) + \vec u(t) \times \vec v(t)$$ ### Integrals $$\int_a^b \vec r(t)dt = \langle \int_a^b f(t)dt, \int_a^b g(t)dt, \int_a^b h(t)dt\rangle $$ If $\vec R(t)$ is an anti-derivative of $\vec r(t)$ (i.e. $\vec R'(t) = \vec r(t)$) then $$ \int_a^b \vec r(t)dt = \vec R(t)|_a^b = \vec R(b) - \vec R(a)$$ ## [Arc Length](arc-len.html) $$ length = \int\limits_a^b|\vec r'(u)|du = \int\limits_a^b \sqrt{\left(f'(t)\right)^2 + \left(g'(t)\right)^2 + \left(h'(t)\right)^2}du$$ $$ s(t) = \int\limits_0^t|\vec r'(u)|du $$ ### Natural Equation $$ \vec r_n(s) = \vec r(t(s))$$ Where $t(s)$ = the inverse of $\int\limits_0^t {|\vec r'(u)|}du$ ## [Curvature](curvature.html) $\kappa$ (curvature) is defined as: $$\kappa = |\vec r_0''(s)|$$ Or (easier to compute): $$\kappa(t) = {|\vec r'(t) \times \vec r''(t)|\over |r'(t)|^3}$$ The concept of first and second drivative can be applied to the physics equation for the movement of a particle: $$\vec a(t) = \vec g$$ $$\vec v(t) = \int a(t) = \vec gt + v_0$$ $$\vec r(t) = \int v(t) = \frac{1}{2}\vec gt^2 + v_0t + p_0$$ $$\vec g = -9.8\hat j$$ ## [Components of Acceleration](acceleration_comp.html) There are two components to the acceleration, one that determines change in speed, and another which determines change in direction. ![Image Clarification](accel_comp.jpg) ### Tangential - speed It has the same direction as velocity, and is the component of acceleration with respect to the velocity (vector projection of acceleration on velocity) ### Normal - direction It is perpendicular to velocity, and is the component of acceleration with respect to vector perpendicular to velocity ### Application It seems to help define acceleration in another way: $$\nu = |\vec v(t)|$$ $$\vec N(t) = {T'(t)\over |T'(t)|}$$ $$\vec a (t) = \vec v'(t) = \nu'(t) * T + \nu(t) * T'(t)$$ $$a(t) = \nu'T + \kappa*\nu^2N$$
